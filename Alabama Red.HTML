Habitat & Range in Alabama

The Alabama red-bellied turtle is a freshwater species that thrives in rivers, streams, and bays with lots of aquatic plants, which are its main food source.

Its range is extremely limited. It is found almost exclusively in the fresh and brackish waters of the Mobile-Tensaw River Delta in Mobile and Baldwin counties. This small, specific habitat makes the species very vulnerable.

Nesting Habits ü•ö

Female turtles leave the water to build their nests in open, sandy areas with plenty of sun exposure. This helps the eggs incubate properly.

  • Nesting Season: Typically from May through July.
  • Process: The female uses her hind feet to dig a flask-shaped hole where she lays her eggs before covering them back up.
  • Clutch Size: A female can lay several clutches per season, with each clutch containing 4 to 20 eggs.
  • Threats: Nests are highly vulnerable to predators like raccoons, alligators, and fire ants. Human development and flooding can also destroy nests.

Movement & Tracking üë£

In Water: They are powerful swimmers, using their webbed feet to navigate the river currents. They spend most of their lives in the water, feeding on aquatic vegetation.

On Land: Their movement on land is slow and deliberate. They typically only leave the water to bask in the sun on logs or to nest.

Footprints: Turtle tracks can help in creating tracking models. The prints from their webbed feet often look like fan-shaped impressions in sand or mud. Key features include:

  • Five distinct toe/claw marks fanning out.
  • Alternating tracks from the front and back feet.
  • A possible "drag mark" down the center from the tail.

Conservation Status: Endangered

The Alabama red-bellied turtle is listed as an endangered species by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service. The primary threats to its survival include:

  • Habitat Degradation: Pollution and development in the Mobile-Tensaw Delta harm the water quality and reduce food sources.
  • Nest Predation: High rates of predation on eggs and hatchlings limit population growth.
  • Bycatch: Turtles can get caught and drown in fishing gear like crab traps.
  • Boat Strikes: Collisions with boats can injure or kill adult turtles.
