Kemp's Ridley Sea Turtle

Lepidochelys kempii

The World's Most Endangered Sea Turtle

Who is the Kemp's Ridley?

  • Conservation Status: Critically Endangered
  • Size: Smallest sea turtle! Adults are ~2 feet long.
  • Weight: 75 - 100 pounds.
  • Lifespan: Estimated 30 to 50 years.
  • Diet: Carnivores. They love to eat crabs, clams, and shrimp, using their powerful beaks to crush shells.

Habitat: A Coastal Dweller

Kemp's Ridleys prefer warm, shallow coastal waters with sandy or muddy bottoms, perfect for finding their favorite food.

  • Primary Range: The Gulf of Mexico is their main home.
  • Juvenile Habitat: Young turtles can be found along the U.S. Atlantic coast.
  • Depth: They are rarely found in waters deeper than 160 feet.

Our Local Connection

Alabama's coastal waters are a critical foraging ground for Kemp's Ridleys, especially juveniles.

  • Where: Found seasonally in Mobile Bay, Perdido Bay, and the Mississippi Sound.
  • When: Most common from April through October.
  • Importance: Protecting these feeding areas is vital for the survival of the species.

The Amazing "Arribada"

Kemp's Ridleys are famous for their unique, synchronized mass nesting behavior called an "arribada" (Spanish for "arrival"). Over 95% of nesting occurs on one beach in Rancho Nuevo, Mexico, where thousands of females come ashore together to lay eggs.

How They Move

In the Water: Graceful Flyers

They use long, powerful front flippers like wings to "fly" through the water. Rear flippers act as rudders for steering.

On Land: Awkward Crawlers

They use an alternating flipper gait, pulling their body weight along. This is a key feature for identifying their tracks!

Tracking a Kemp's Ridley

Your AI model can learn to identify their unique tracks (crawls) in the sand to help conservationists.

  • Gait Pattern: Asymmetrical (alternating) crawl pattern.
  • Width: The crawl is narrow, about 28-35 inches wide.
  • Flipper Marks: Comma-shaped slashes, alternating side-to-side.
  • Tail Drag: A distinct line may be visible down the center.

Threats & Conservation

Bycatch

Accidental capture in fishing gear is the #1 threat.

Habitat Loss

Coastal development and pollution destroy feeding areas.

Vessel Strikes

Collisions with boats are a major danger in coastal waters.

Conservation Efforts

Groups like the Alabama Coastal Foundation and programs requiring Turtle Excluder Devices (TEDs) on fishing nets are making a huge difference in protecting them.
