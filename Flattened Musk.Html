Flattened Musk Turtle

An Alabama Endemic Species

Learn More

About the Species

The Flattened Musk Turtle (Sternotherus depressus) is a small freshwater turtle, only growing to about 3-4 inches. Its name comes from its uniquely flat top shell (carapace).

Primarily aquatic, they are known for the musky odor they release from glands when they feel threatened. The shell is typically a yellowish-brown with dark, web-like patterns.

Conservation Status: Threatened

Habitat & Distribution

This turtle requires clean, clear, medium-to-large streams with rocky or sandy bottoms. They spend much of their time hiding under large, flat rocks and submerged logs.

Their entire global population is found only in the upper Black Warrior River system in north-central Alabama.

This limited range makes them extremely vulnerable to habitat degradation.

Nesting & Locomotion

Nesting Habits

From May to July, females travel upland to lay small clutches of 1-4 eggs in sunny, open areas with sandy soil.

How They Move

They are primarily bottom-walkers, crawling along the stream bed instead of swimming in open water. Their flat shape is perfect for navigating under rocks to find food and hide from predators.

Learn More & Get Involved

The biggest threats to the Flattened Musk Turtle are water pollution and habitat destruction from sedimentation. Protecting their habitat is key to their survival.
