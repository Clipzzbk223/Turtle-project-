The Gopher Tortoise

An Alabama Keystone Species

Interactive Presentation for High School AI

About the Species

The Gopher Tortoise (Gopherus polyphemus) is a large, land-dwelling turtle known for its elephant-like back legs and flattened front limbs adapted for digging. They can live for over 60 years!

It's a keystone species, meaning its burrows provide shelter for over 350 other species, including the indigo snake, gopher frog, and various insects. Its presence is crucial for the health of its ecosystem.

In Alabama, it is listed as Threatened under the Endangered Species Act, primarily due to habitat loss.

Habitat & Alabama Range

Ideal Habitat

Gopher tortoises thrive in longleaf pine savannas and other sandy, open-canopied forests. They need well-drained, sandy soils for digging their extensive burrows and low-growing vegetation like wiregrass and legumes for food.

Where to Find Them in Alabama

They are primarily found in the southern counties of Alabama, especially in the Lower Coastal Plain. Key populations exist in Conecuh National Forest and other protected lands in Mobile, Baldwin, Washington, Covington, and Geneva counties.

Life in the Burrow

The Burrow

A tortoise digs a burrow that can be up to 40 feet long and 10 feet deep! The characteristic half-moon shape of the entrance is a key identifier. The burrow maintains a stable temperature and humidity, protecting the tortoise from heat, cold, and fire.

Nesting Habits

Females lay about 5-8 eggs, typically in the sandy mound at the entrance of their burrow (the "apron"). Nesting season in Alabama is usually from May to July. Hatchlings emerge in the fall and are highly vulnerable to predators like raccoons and fire ants.

Movement

Gopher tortoises are slow-moving but deliberate. They forage for food within a home range around their burrow. Their powerful, shovel-like front legs are used for digging, while their sturdy, columnar back legs support their weight as they walk.

Data for Your Tracking Model

To build an AI tracking model, you need to recognize the tortoise and its signs. Here are key visual identifiers.

Footprints

Gopher tortoise tracks are distinctive. The front feet leave a wide, C-shaped track, while the hind feet leave a more rounded impression. Look for a central drag mark left by the tail or the lower shell (plastron) scraping the ground between the tracks.

Other Signs

Burrow Apron

The large, fan-shaped mound of sand at the burrow entrance is a clear indicator. It's often bare of vegetation due to constant use.

Scat

Tortoise scat (droppings) is often found near the burrow. It's fibrous and cylindrical, containing undigested plant matter. Its presence confirms recent activity.
